Why has UK labour productivity stagnated since the Global Financial Crisis?

Aims

Since the Global Financial Crisis of 2008, UK labour productivity has lagged behind a number of other developed economies, and crucially has grown at a significantly lower rate than the pre-2008 trend. This project aims to explore the extent of this so called 'Productivity Puzzle', and examine the potential causes of this fall in productivity growth. I will examine data from across the world on productivity, investment and industrial policy, and demonstrate that the UK is facing structural issues that underpin our dissapointing labour productivity.

The Problem

The chart below uses data from the ONS. UK productivity data is not available from the ONS API so this has been accessed from an excel download, through python. I have cleaned the data and used sklearn to conduct a linear regression on data from before 2008. This shows a clear trend from before the Global Financial Crisis. The UK has clearly decoupled from its previous trend of steady productivity growth. The 'gap' in productivity we are facing is significant, and has serious ramifications for British standards of living, and our competitivenss on the global stage. Global Investment

Capital investment is a key contributor to labour productivity; in general we would expect a greater capital to labour ratio to correspond to higher productivity as workers have a greater level of real capital to work with.

The chart below takes data from the world bank on gross fixed capital formation. I used a batch downloader to access data for each country and combine them to one dataframe. The python notebook can be accessed here.

The data is quoted as percentage of GDP, this allows for comparison between countries and gives an indication of the capital-output ratio across countries. From the data we can see that the UK has had consistently lower capital formation than many other developed economies. Countries such as the US, France and Germany generally have capital formation of 20% or above, whereas the UK has seen rates as low as 15%, as in 2012.

Why does this matter?

The link between investment and productivity has not always been strong. There are many other factors that impact productivity such as R&D spending, or exogenous shocks. In the chart below I have conducted seperate regressions on 4 different periods, in an attempt to control some of these exogenous factors. What is evident is a strong correlation between capital investment and productivity since 2008. This chart also uses World Bank data on the 10 largest European economies. I have used sklearn to conduct linear regressions, the python notebook can be accessed here. The graph is also interactive: scroll to zoom in on individual graphs.

Industry Analysis

Another potential cause of low productivity is a shift from high to low productivity sectors. I have taken data from the ONS on employment levels and productivity by sector. I have calculated some additional metrics on python to allow for better analysis. Specifically, relative productivity refers to how productive a sector is compared with the national average (i.e a value of 1). The data highlights a significant shift away from manufacturing and wholesale trade, which have remained as the most productive sectors since 2007.

Challenges

Finding data has been relatively easy due to the nature of my project: macroeconomic indicators are freely published by national and global bodies such as the ONS, Eurostat and the World Bank.

I faced some difficulties cleaning and utilising the ONS industry productivity data. The data was taken from excel files, which warrants a basic level of cleaning, however there was more significant difficulty when merging datasets as the labelling and formatting of different British industries varies across datasets. My solution was to label all the industries with SIC 2007 codes using a python dictionary; some rows had to be summed due to groupings of different industries, however I successfully managed to merge my required datasets using industry codes so that I could compare employment levels and productivity.

Another issue I have faced in this project is inconsistency in measuring productivity. UK data is not available on Eurostat; the UK measures output per hour by index of GBP whereas the EU obviously measures in index or Euros. For my comparison across countries I chose to use an index for one graph (this required re-indexing due again to differences between Eurostat and ONS) and output per worker for the other chart, as the World Bank only measures output per worker.
