Why has UK labour productivity stagnated since the Global Financial Crisis?

Since the Global Financial Crisis of 2008, UK labour producivity has lagged behind a number of other developed economies, and crucially has grown at a significantly lower rate than the pre-2008 trend. This project aims to explore the extent of this so called 'Productivity Puzzle', and examine the potential causes of this fall in productivity growth.

The Problem

The chart below uses data from the ONS. Unfortunately ONS productivity data is not available from an API; I have used python to access the excel file containing productivity data. I have cleaned the data and used sklearn to conduct a linear regression on data from before 2008. This gives a linear trend from before the Global Financial Crisis. Overlaying the fitted line on top of the actual data shows a clear decoupling of output per hour from the pre-2008 trend rate. The python notebook can be accessed here.

European Productivity

It is important to consider how productivity has developed in other countries to see if the UK really is an outlier. The chart below takes data from the ONS and Eurostat on GDP per hour worked. I have re-indexed the data to 1995 as the base year so that the recent development in productivity is clear. The python notebook can be accessed here The graph is interactive: filter for countries by clicking on legend elements, or the lines themselves; drag across the lower chart to select the time frame. The data seems to suggest we are not alone. Other countries have also faced low growth. However be aware this is a chart showing growth from an index year. It does not show the true difference in labour productivity between countries.

Global Investment

Capital investment is a key contributor to labour productivity; in general we would expect a greater capital to labour ratio to correspond to higher productivity as workers have a greater level of real capital to work with. The chart below takes data from the world bank on gross fixed capital formation. I used a batch downloader to access data for each country and combine them to one dataframe. The python notebook can be accessed here. The data is quoted as percentage of GDP, this allows for comparison between countries and gives an indication of the capital-output ratio across countries. From the data we can see that the UK has had consistently lower capital formation than many other developed economies. Countries such as the US, France and Germany generally have capital formation of 20% or above, whereas the UK has seen rates as low as 15% in 2012.
